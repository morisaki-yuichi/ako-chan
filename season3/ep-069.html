The Idea

That night, the boy opened the Pattern A page.

Eight entries. Four cities. He had tried seven methods. Each one had found the same gap.

He took a clean page and wrote the problem at the top.

Pattern A. 8 instances. 4 cities. Explanation: none.

He sat with the blank space below it.

Two possible approaches.

The first was simple. He could not record Pattern A with his own senses. He needed an external device — something that could observe and record what he could not. A device without his gap.

The second approach was different. He thought through the logic carefully. If Pattern A was not random, something was arranging it. If the arrangement came from a decision already made — from a point further along the line than he was now — then the pattern itself was evidence that such things were possible. He opened to a new page and began to calculate.

He stopped.

He read what he had written. He sat for a long time.

Then he turned to the next page.

Device first. Then: together.

The pages with the second calculation stayed where they were. He did not cross them out.

He opened a clean section of the notebook and picked up his pencil.

He began to draw.
