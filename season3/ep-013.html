Season 3 · Ep 13

First Asymmetry

The next morning, the boy sat at his desk and opened his notebook.

He turned back to the pier section. He had written three full pages that day — crane movements, tide readings, bus times. He found the pier entries and read them slowly.

The corgi: eight lines. He had written the size, the position near the step, the way it stood. He had noted one sound it made.

The tortoise: four lines. Movement across the concrete. Direction. Distance.

The woman: one line.

He looked at the three entries. Same location. Same time. Same group. Two with thick records. One with almost nothing.

The method had been the same. He had opened his notebook for all three. The corgi's data had come easily. The tortoise's data had come easily. The woman's entry had stopped at one line.

He turned to a clean page. At the top, he wrote one word.

Asymmetry.

Below it, he wrote the three numbers: eight lines, four lines, one line.

He looked at the page for a while. It was not a conclusion. It was only a shape.

He closed the notebook.
